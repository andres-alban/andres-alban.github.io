Model of sequential trial with covariates

  • Patients \(t=1,2, \ldots, T\) enrolled sequentially
  • Trial manager observes patient with covariates \( \Xt{t}\in\Xset\subseteq\R^{\Xdim} \), assignes to her treatment \( \Wt{t}\in\Wset=\{1,2,\ldots,\Wdim\} \). Outcome \( \Yt{t}\in\Yset\subseteq\R \) observed after delay \( \delay \)
  • History \( \Ht{t} \) represents all available data at time \( t \)
  • Allocation decision \( (\pi_{t})_{t = 1,2,\ldots,T}: (\Xt{t},\Ht{t-1}) \to \Wt{t} \)
  • At time \( T+\delay \) trial manager picks the treatment strategy for post-trial patients: \( \fadopt{}(\Xgen) = \argmax_{\Wpostgen\in\Wset}\E[\r_{\cfull}(\Xgen,\Wpostgen) \mid \Ht{T+\delay}] \)

Model of sequential trial with covariates

  • Linear response function \( \respfnt{t}= \E[\Yt{t}\mid \cfull,\Xt{t},\Wt{t}] \):
\begin{align*} \respfn{\cfull} &= \celem{0,0}+\sum_{i=1}^{\Wdim}\underbrace{\indic{\Wgen=i}\celem{i,0}}_{\text{treatment effect}}+\sum_{l=1}^{\Xdim}\underbrace{\Xgenelem{l} \celem{0,l}}_{\text{prognostic term}}+\sum_{i=1}^{\Wdim}\sum_{l=1}^{\Xdim}\underbrace{\indic{\Wgen=i}\Xgenelem{l}\celem{i,l}}_{\text{predictive term}} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \respfn{\cfull} &= \celem{0,0}{\labelem{0,0}}+\sum_{i=1}^{\Wdim}\underbrace{\indic{\Wgen=i}\celem{i,0}}_{\text{treatment effect}}{\cdot \labelem{i,0}}+\sum_{l=1}^{\Xdim}\underbrace{\Xgenelem{l} \celem{0,l}}_{\text{prognostic term}}{\cdot \labelem{0,l}}+\sum_{i=1}^{\Wdim}\sum_{l=1}^{\Xdim}\underbrace{\indic{\Wgen=i}\Xgenelem{l}\celem{i,l}}_{\text{predictive term}}{\cdot \labelem{i,l}}\\ &=(\Xgen\kron\Wgen)\c \end{align*} Labeling \( \lab \): if label \( \labelem{i,l}=0 \), then \( \cfullelem{i,l}=0 \); if \( \labelem{i,l}=1 \), then \( \cfullelem{i,l} \) is potentially active
  • Bayesian linear regression to learn coefficients:
\begin{align*} \Yt{t}\mid \c,\Xt{t},\Wt{t}&\overset{\mathrm{i.i.d.}}{\sim}\Normal((\Wt{t}\kron \Xt{t}) \c, \sigma^{2})\\ \c&\sim \Normal(\ct{0},\Sigma_{0}) \end{align*}

Summary of theory

  • Optimal policy is solvable through dynamic programming (but generally computationally infeasible)
  • We extend Expected Value of Information (EVI) methods to develop the fEVI policy to maximize offline rewards and the fEVIon policy to maximize online rewards
  • We prove the one-step look-ahead and asymptotic optimality of fEVI
  • We develop a heuristic based on the Lasso technique to adaptively infer the labeling

Trial value and optimal policy

  • At time \( t=T+\delay \) the trial concludes; post-trial patient with covariates \( \Xpostt{i} \) receives treatment \( \Wpostt{i} \) and obtains health outcome \( \Ypostt{i} \)
  • Trial value with allocation policy \( \pi \) (offline rewards): \( V^{\pi}= \E^{\pi}\left[\r_{\cfull}(\Xpostt{1},\fadopt{}(\Xpostt{1})) \right] \)
  • Optimal value to go: \begin{aligned} V_{t}(\Kgen)&= \sup_{\pi_{t+1}}\E^{\pi_{t+1}}[ V_{t+1}(\upt{t+1}(\Kgen,\Xt{t+1},\Wt{t+1},\Yt{t-\delay+1})) \mid \Kt{t}=\Kgen], \text{ for}~ t = 0,\ldots,T-1 \\ V_{T}(\Kgen)&= \E[\max_{\Wpostgen}(\Wpostgen \kron \Xpostt{1})\ct{T+\delay}\mid \Kt{T}=\Kgen] . \end{aligned}
Proposition
The DP satisfies \( V_{0}(\Kt{0})=\sup_{\pi\in\Pi}{V^\pi} \). There exists a deterministic Markov allocation policy \( \pi^{*} \), which can be computed using Bellman's recursion, that solves \( \max_{\pi\in\Pi}V^{\pi} \).

EVI heuristics for learning with predictive and prognostic covariates

  • EVI: Expected Value of Information
  • fEVI: makes sampling decision that maximizes EVI of one more sample
    • Extends EVI/KG approach of Bayesian optimization (Frazier et al., 2008; Chick & Inoue, 2001) to pred/prog covariates
    • Assigns the treatment with the highest fEVI index: \begin{align*} \nu_{t}(\Xgen,\Wgen)&= \underbrace{\E\Big[\max_{\Wpostgen\in\Wset}(\Wpostgen \kron \Xpostt{1}) \ct{t+\delay+1} \mid \Ht{t},\Xt{t+1}=\Xgen,\Wt{t+1}=\Wgen\Big]}_{\text{implement after one more patient and pipeline clears}}\\ &- \underbrace{\E\Big[\max_{\Wpostgen\in\Wset}(\Wpostgen \kron \Xpostt{1}) \ct{t+\delay} \mid \Ht{t}\Big]}_{\text{implement after pipeline clears}} \end{align*}

Theoretical properties of the fEVI policy

Proposition : One-step look-ahead optimality
For sample size \( T=1 \) fEVI is optimal.
Theorem
If an allocation policy samples the treatment with the highest fEVI index with probability bounded away from 0, then it is asymptotically optimal.
Usual techniques for this proof: show each treatment is sampled infinitely often. Not sufficient in our setting.
Key steps of proof:
  • For any policy, posterior parameters \( \ct{t} \) and \( \Sigma_{t} \) converge
  • For a policy sampling the largest fEVI treatment with \( >0 \) probability: if it did not reach a state with no value in further sampling, the posterior parameters would not converge
  • A policy that reaches a state with no value in further sampling is asymptotically optimal
Conclusion: fEVI is optimal for small and large sample sizes

Metrics and comparators

Expected Opportunity Cost: difference in value between an oracle and the policy (offline rewards)
\begin{equation*} \EOC^{\pi}(\c;T) \defined \E^{\pi}\left[ \max_{\Wgen\in\Wset}(\Wgen\kron\Xpostt{1})\c - (\fadopt{\ct{T+\delay}}(\Xpostt{1})\kron\Xpostt{1})\c \mid \c \right] \end{equation*}
Cumulative regret: cumulative value difference between an oracle and the policy (online rewards)
\begin{equation*} \textup{cum. regret}^{\pi}(\c;T) \defined \sum_{t+1}^{T} \E^{\pi}\left[ \max_{\Wgen\in\Wset}(\Wgen\kron\Xt{1})\c - (\fadopt{\ct{T+\delay}}(\Xt{t})\kron\Xpostt{1})\c \mid \c \right] \end{equation*}
Comparator policies:
  • Thompson sampling (Thompson, 1933) , Top-two Thompson sampling (Russo, 2020)
  • BC* (adaptation of Biased Coin scheme, Pocock & Simon, 1975 )
  • RABC (Response Adaptive Biased Coin, (Zhao et al., 2022) )
  • Random

Inference of labeling

  • Instead of fixing a labeling based on medical expertise, use the available data to learn it
  • Use Lasso every few iterations to learn which labels are inactive
  • Select the penalty factor using cross-validation
Labelings
  • all-pred: all covariates are predictive
  • all-prog: all covariates are prognostic
  • known: labeling is known
  • Lasso(min): cross-validation minimizes error
  • Lasso(-1se): cross-validation is within 1se of the minimum error

Summary

Key take-away
  • Theoretical and computational results: there is benefit to adaptive contextual learning with EVI-based approach in settings with predictive and prognostic covariates
Extensions: additional conceptual and practical considerations
  • Online rewards
  • Inference of labeling if unknown
  • Adaptive stopping times
  • Dynamic enrollment decisions
  • Heteroskedastic or unknown patient outcome variance; 0-1 outcomes; surrogate measures (available before final primary outcome); fixed patent protection period
